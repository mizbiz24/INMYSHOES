Introduction · Part One Before Any of This Makes Sense Dean

I don't think I ever wanted to be invisible. It wasn't a choice, or a decision, or even something I noticed happening in real time. It just settled into me. The way certain things do — without asking, without warning, until one day it's just part of who you are.

Somewhere along the way, I learned that being seen wasn't the same as being understood. And once that realization takes hold — once it really digs in — stepping back starts to feel less like giving up and more like protecting yourself.

There's a kind of quiet that comes with it. Not the heavy, suffocating kind. Something softer. More controlled. You stop performing. You stop trying to explain yourself to people who were never really listening. You exist at the edges of rooms — present, unbothering. Close enough to belong somewhere. Never quite the center of anything.

And more importantly — you stop having to deal with what happens when people leave.

Because they do.

Not always dramatically. Not always in ways that make clean sense.

But eventually — always eventually — they do.

· · ·
OneMy Dad Left First

It wasn't a big dramatic exit. No slammed doors. No screaming match I could point to later and say — that was the moment everything broke. If anything it was almost careful. Like he'd rehearsed how to remove himself from our lives in a way that caused the least possible disruption. The way you'd move a piece of furniture without scuffing the floor.

There was another woman. That part didn't need to be said out loud to be understood. He told me it wouldn't be forever. That he just needed to fix something — in himself, in his head, somewhere I couldn't see. He said he'd come back once everything made sense again. He looked at me when he said it. Really looked, the way people do when they want to be remembered a certain way.

I didn't ask what he meant.

I think I already understood.

· · ·
TwoThe New Normal

After that, things didn't collapse. They unraveled. Slowly. In ways that never demanded your attention but changed everything anyway. My mom tried at first — kept the routines going, answered calls, filled the silence with small practical things. But something in her shifted. Gradually enough that you couldn't name when it started.

I started waking up at night to sounds from downstairs. Nothing alarming. Just enough to pull me out of sleep and make me listen. I learned which stairs creaked and which didn't. I learned how to be a shadow.

From the top of the landing, you could see part of the living room. Not everything. Just enough. She'd be there — sometimes on the floor, back against the couch. A bottle within reach, like a detail in a sentence that reframes everything else.

It wasn't chaotic the way people expect. It was worse than that. It was consistent.

Some nights she said his name. Some nights nothing. And sometimes she'd notice me in the dark.

Mom
"Don't be like him."
Mom
"Don't turn into your father."

I never figured out what she meant. Whether she was warning me against leaving or against staying wrong. I never asked. I don't think she expected me to.

And then she left too. Cleaner than my dad's version. There was a conversation. She needed time, she said. Space to fix herself. It wouldn't take long. It was temporary.

I nodded.

I had heard that before.

· · ·
ThreeLola's House

After that, it was just me and my grandmother. And somehow — it wasn't bad. It was quiet in a different way. Less fragile. More load-bearing. The kind of quiet that didn't feel like a pause before something breaks. It just stayed. Not healed. Not fixed. Just settled — the way sediment does after water finally goes still.

I think that's when I understood what my place was going to be. Not the one people stay for. The one who stays.

· · ·
FourUseful

It didn't happen all at once. Just kept occurring the way patterns do when no one interrupts them. They'd sit beside me. Start talking. I'd stay. I listened — not because I had answers, but because I didn't leave. I cycled through the same quiet reassurances: That's just how it is. You'll get through it. I'm here.

People don't always come looking for answers. They come carrying weight — things they've been holding too long with nowhere to put them. And I was there. Available. A place where things could be set down.

There's a difference between being important and being useful. I learned that early. I wasn't important. But I was useful — like a hallway. A place people moved through on their way somewhere else.

· · ·
FiveKeith

Out of everyone who eventually found a way to leave, there was one person who didn't.

Keith.

He wasn't supposed to be different. We met as kids — not close, not yet. Just two people in the same place at the same time. I stayed where it was quiet. He didn't. He sat beside me. No reason. No preamble. Just — there. He talked. I listened. Most people, after registering the quality of my silences, eventually redirected themselves toward someone more reciprocal.

He didn't.

I understood it more clearly one afternoon on the walk home. We weren't talking about anything important — he was a few steps ahead, narrating something I was only half-following, and I was doing the thing where I trail behind without really deciding to. Then a dog came through an open gate. One sharp bark and I stopped.

Keith moved — stepped directly in front of me without hesitating, without drama, the way you do when the decision's already been made somewhere below conscious thought.

Keith
"Hey — don't. Just stay there."

His voice was quiet. Unhurried. The dog barked again. Keith crouched and picked up a small rock from the ground.

Keith
"Go."

Nothing happened. A long suspended beat. And then — with a sincerity I still can't fully explain — Keith made a small sound under his breath.

...Arf.

Even the dog looked confused. Keith didn't react to himself. Didn't flinch, didn't laugh. Just kept looking at the dog with the calm authority of someone who had committed completely to a strategy and would not be revising it.

Keith
"Go."

And this time — it worked. The dog backed away and disappeared. Keith turned around.

Keith
"You okay?"
Dean
"Yeah."
Keith
"Alright."

No callback. No retelling it later. He just kept walking. I fell into step beside him like nothing happened. That was it. That was the whole thing. When something needed doing, he did it. When it was done, he let it be done.

He didn't leave.

It takes time to get used to someone staying. At first you don't trust it — you keep waiting for the shift, the distance, the slow fade that always comes eventually. But when it doesn't come, you stop bracing for it. You let it just exist. Because the moment you start asking why someone stayed is usually the moment they don't anymore.

And now, years later, he was still there.

Introduction · Part Two The Ones Who Can't Afford to Stop Carlo

There's a version of strength that looks like keeping going. Not the dramatic kind — not the kind that makes speeches or breaks down and rebuilds in the third act. Just the quiet, structural kind. The kind that shows up for every shift, counts the change correctly, says something kind to every person who walks through the door, and then goes home and does it again tomorrow.

Carlo had that kind. I noticed it the way I notice most things — slowly, then all at once. At first he was just the guy behind the register at the convenience store Keith dragged me to every other week. Blue polo. Dark circles. The particular expression of someone who has processed enough interactions to move through them efficiently without being rude about it.

But the more I watched, the more I understood: I was looking at someone holding a life together with both hands and not telling anyone about it.

· · ·
OneThe Schedule

Carlo moved through his shifts with the efficiency of someone who had learned to compress rest into small margins — the two minutes between customers when he'd roll his shoulder and close his eyes briefly, not dramatically, just the way you reboot something that's been running too long. The way he'd eat standing up during lulls. The way, when it was slow, he'd pull out a notebook — not for anything creative, just numbers. Calculations. The arithmetic of getting through a month.

TwoWhat Lola Remembers

Carlo grew up with his grandmother. The longer version involves a road, an accident, and the specific kind of loss that happens when you're young enough that grief doesn't have language yet — it's just an absence that becomes architectural, something you build your whole life around without realizing. His parents died when he was a kid. His lola raised him. And for a long time, that was enough.

Then the Alzheimer's came. It doesn't happen all at once. That's the thing about it that breaks people most — not the ending, but the slowness. The fog that arrives in increments so small you can argue with yourself for months about whether it's really happening. And then, eventually, the fog is just where they live now.

Keith mentioned once that Carlo had told him — Keith just asks people things, it's remarkable — that there are days his lola doesn't know who he is. That she'll look at him with this polite confused expression and say something like, you're a good boy, who did you say you were again?

And Carlo will say his name. And she'll nod like she's filing it away. And sometimes it sticks and sometimes it doesn't.

She's in a home now. A good one — which is why the notebook has all those numbers in it, why the schedule looks the way it does. He is paying for the place where the last person who remembered him as a child is slowly forgetting that he exists. He does this without complaint. He does this like it's just what you do.

I know something about growing up with a grandmother as the whole of your world. The particular security of it, and the particular fragility — the way you understand, even as a kid, that this arrangement has a different kind of ending than the ones other people have. You love them knowing that. You love them anyway.

ThreeMira

There was a girl. Her name was Mira. I didn't know her — I know about her the way I know most things in Carlo's life, from context, from absence, from the small tells people leave behind when they're trying not to show that something happened.

She came up once, sideways, when Lauren made a joke about heartbreak being in aisle two, and Carlo laughed — genuinely, the way you laugh when something is both funny and true — and for just a second, something crossed his face before he put it away. I noticed. I always notice. I just don't always say anything.

The way I imagine it — and I want to be clear, I'm only imagining — is that it wasn't a fight. Carlo didn't seem like someone whose relationships ended in fights. It probably ended quietly, the way things do when both people are too tired for drama. She probably said something true. He probably said something true back. And then there was nothing left, and she left, and he went to work the next morning because what else do you do.

You go to work. You count the change. You make a joke at the register. You keep going. Because the bills don't pause for breakups. The schedule doesn't have a line for grief.

Mira left because he was always somewhere else. And he was always somewhere else because the world he was holding together didn't have room for him to just be present in one place and feel things. That's not a villain origin story. That's just two people and an impossible situation and no one to blame and nowhere for the sadness to go except inward and forward, always forward, never stopping long enough to let it land.

FourWhat I Recognized

I recognized something in him. The specific quality of someone who grew up having to be fine. Who learned early that falling apart was a luxury. Who became the person who didn't need anything, who always had something to offer, who made the joke and handed out the towel and said something kind at the register even at the end of a twelve-hour day.

The tiredness that doesn't look like tiredness because it's been running so long it just looks like personality.

I recognized myself. Not exactly. Not the same. But the shape of it — the outline — was familiar in a way that sat in my chest quietly and didn't move.

There's a kind of courage nobody names because it doesn't look like courage — it looks like just showing up. Just getting through. Just doing the next thing and the thing after that. But I've seen what the alternative looks like from the top of a staircase in the dark, and I know that showing up, again and again, when everything in you wants to not — that takes something real. Something most people don't have and don't know they don't have until they need it.

Carlo had it. He probably didn't know that about himself. The people who have it usually don't. They're too busy keeping going to notice that keeping going is the hard part.

That it's the whole thing.

That it counts.

Introduction · Part Three What It Looks Like When Someone Tries Abby

I think about the different ways people love. There's the loud kind, the kind that announces itself and demands reciprocation and makes a scene when it doesn't get it. And then there's the quiet structural kind — the kind that just shows up, again and again, in small practical ways. The kind that bakes things and remembers things and cheers from bleachers and learns the names of your cousins and asks your aunts how their knees are doing.

Abby loved the second way. She was — and I mean this without any qualification — the best girlfriend anyone in that building had ever seen. Not in the performed sense. The actual kind. The kind that costs something and gives it anyway.

· · ·
OneOwen's Sister

You have to understand Abby in the context of Owen first, because you can't fully understand either of them without the other. Their parents work abroad — the kind of arrangement that starts as temporary and becomes permanent before anyone formally decides. So it was Owen and Abby, mostly. Owen older, Abby younger, figuring out the architecture of an almost-empty house.

Owen played bass and took his knocks with an easy calm that made it look like nothing touched him, even when things did. Abby baked and planned and made lists and showed up for people — not because she'd been told to, but because she'd decided early that showing up was the thing that mattered most and she was going to be someone who did it.

Some people learn to love from being loved. And some people learn to love from the absence of it — from the silence of a house where the people who were supposed to be there aren't, and you decide that when you love someone, you're going to make sure they feel it. You're going to make absolutely sure.

TwoVince

His name was Vince. I want to be clear about this from the beginning: Vince wasn't a bad person. That would make this story easier to tell. But the truth is he was fine — genuinely fine, good even, the kind of person you root for when you're watching from outside. Kind. Ambitious. He made Abby laugh in that way where she forgot she was performing anything and was just laughing.

Abby was at every game. She knew his class schedule better than he did. She baked for his study groups, remembered every professor he'd complained about, made sure there was food when he forgot to eat. She charmed every tita at every family gathering. She didn't do it to be impressive. She did it because she meant it. That's the part that matters.

ThreeThe Scholarship

Then the scholarship came. Abroad. Full ride. And Abby knew before he'd even finished telling her. She could see the whole trajectory, the opportunity, the version of his future that this unlocked. She told him to go. Not reluctantly. Not with conditions. She told him to go the way she did everything — fully, without half-measures, with the complete weight of what she meant behind it.

He went. And Abby stayed, and waited, and loved him across twelve time zones with the same completeness she'd loved him across a classroom.

There's a particular kind of brave that doesn't get named. It's not the running-into-burning-buildings kind. It's the staying kind. The watching someone you love walk toward their future and being genuinely happy for them while something in you quietly reorganizes itself around the new shape of their absence.

FourThe Calls

At first, it was fine. More than fine — the way long-distance things can be at the beginning, when the distance is still novel enough to make every call feel significant. She kept her phone charged, volume up. She knew his schedule in the new time zone. She was always ready to pick up.

She adjusted every time. Told herself it was the adjustment period. That what they had was strong enough to survive gaps — and she believed it, not naively, but because she'd made it strong. She'd done the work. She had receipts.

What nobody told her was that you can do everything right and it still might not be enough. Not because what you built wasn't real. But because the person on the other side has to be doing the work too.

The cruelest thing about being the perfect girlfriend is that you get really good at absorbing the signs. You learn to translate I've been busy as I'm still here. You become fluent in the language of diminishment, and you use that fluency to convince yourself that things are fine, that you're not losing something, that you're just being understanding, which is what love is, which is what you signed up for.

FiveWhat I See

She takes care of everyone. Lauren when the truck soaks her folder. Owen when the near-miss sends the whole store into a brief alarm — she's first to the door, first to call out his name, first to exhale when he says he's okay. Carlo when he's been at that counter for nine hours and she makes him laugh once, genuinely.

She takes care of everyone and she does it without asking anything back. And the specific quiet tragedy of Abby is that the person she's been waiting for to show up for her — with the same completeness, the same consistency, the same you-matter energy she brings to everyone she loves — that person keeps calling less and less.

And she keeps picking up anyway. Volume up. Phone charged. Always ready.

The saddest thing isn't being unloved. The saddest thing is being the kind of person who knows exactly how to love and having that precision mean nothing in the end — not because it wasn't real, not because it wasn't enough, but because love isn't algebra. Doing everything right doesn't solve for x. It just means you did everything right.

She's been doing that her whole life.

For everyone.

Nobody's done it for her yet.

Introduction · Part Four Some People Enter a Room. Lauren Arrives. Lauren

Lauren, I noticed from the very first time we ended up in the same space, was someone who announced everything. Not loudly. Not in the way that demands attention. But in the way that some people just carry their whole self with them everywhere they go, and the room rearranges itself slightly to make room. She walked like she had a playlist running. She talked like she was always mid-sentence in a conversation that had been going on for years.

She had a folder. Always. The kind with plastic sleeves, color-coded tabs, a schedule in the front pocket. It became, over time, an extension of her — physical proof that she was organized, prepared, always somewhere to be and a plan for getting there.

It was only later that I understood what the folder actually was. A prop. Not in the bad way. In the way that some people carry something physical just to have something to hold.

TwoThe Part She Doesn't Talk About

Here's the thing about being the funny one, the loud one, the one with the energy and the folder and the infectious laugh: nobody asks how you're doing. Not really. Because you always seem fine.

Lauren was — and I'm going to say this the way she would want it said, which is plainly — bigger than what the dramas said the lead was supposed to look like. She knew it. Of course she knew it. She'd make the joke before anyone else could — something self-aware, something that acknowledged it before it could be used against her. She was good at that. The strategic redirect. The preemptive laugh.

There's a kind of armor that looks like confidence. It fits perfectly. It's very convincing. The only way to tell it from the real thing is that the real thing doesn't need to be worn — it just is. Armor, you have to put on every morning.

She wanted the K-drama story. The bus stop. The rain. The person who finally sees her the way she's been waiting to be seen. She just didn't fully believe it was available to her. Not because she wasn't worth it. But because nobody had ever made her feel like she was.

Not yet.

ThreeHis Name Was Rein

I'm going to tell this part carefully, because Lauren deserves that. His name was Rein. Student council president, consistent dean's lister, the kind of person who walked into rooms and immediately became the reference point. He was, objectively, the male lead of someone's story. Just — it turned out that story and Lauren's story were not the same story.

It started incidentally. They ended up near each other because of an event, or a project. He remembered something she'd mentioned in passing — just brought it up later, naturally, like she was someone whose details were worth retaining.

That was it. That was the whole thing. For Lauren, it was enough.

The problem with being someone who loves things fully is that you apply the same intensity to everything. The comeback countdown. The fan meet. The one afternoon when a boy looked at you like you were a person worth paying attention to.

The hardest part about one-sided things isn't the rejection. It's the waiting. The way you keep giving weight to moments that were weightless for the other person. The way you construct a whole story out of material that was never meant to be a story.

She didn't look like the lead of the story she was living in.

But she felt every single frame of it.

Introduction · Part Five The Boy Who Put Things Down Owen

There's a specific kind of person who never had the luxury of falling apart. Not because they were built differently. Not because they didn't feel things. But because the circumstances of their life had arranged themselves in such a way that falling apart was simply never scheduled. There were always more pressing things on the calendar. Someone to drive. Someone to feed. A younger person watching from a doorway, waiting to see if the floor was still going to hold.

Owen is one of those people.

I knew of him before I knew him. He was already a story people told — the student council president who actually showed up, the bassist who turned a three-piece into something that sounded like real music, the guy who at twenty-two had already lived enough for someone's entire novel. The first time I saw him with Abby, the thing I noticed was the way his whole body calibrated to wherever she was in the room. Not hovering, not anxious. Just — aware. The default orientation of a person who has spent years making sure someone else is okay.

· · ·
OneWhat He Came From

Owen and Abby's parents left the year he was seventeen. Not dramatically. They went abroad — the way people say it, like the word makes it smaller than it is. There were plans. Money sent home. Video calls on Sundays that got shorter until they were mostly just proof of concept.

Abby was fourteen. She told me once she doesn't remember the exact day they left. She just remembers that one day Owen was her kuya who played bass in the garage at night — and the next day he was the one who signed her permission slips and argued with the landlord and knew how to cook three things.

I think about the specific violence of a responsibility that doesn't ask permission before it arrives. Owen didn't apply for the job. Nobody interviewed him. It just appeared — fully formed — on a morning when he was still seventeen and still figuring out who he was going to be. He picked it up. Because the alternative was Abby not being okay. You don't weigh that option against the other one. You just pick it up.

Some people grow up. Owen just became responsible. Those aren't the same thing — but from the outside, they look identical, and most people don't check which one they're looking at.

TwoSteel Strain

Before all of it — before the president title and the permission slips — Owen had a band. He named it himself. Steel Strain. Three people, three instruments, the idea of something load-bearing. He was proud of that name — quietly, without announcing it, the way he was proud of anything.

Their first real gig. Small venue. Owen had taped their name to the equipment case in careful block letters. The host took the stage, looked at his clipboard, made a decision with his eyes that he should not have been allowed to make.

Host
"Please welcome to the stage — Still Trying!"

The room cheered. Owen stared at his banner from backstage. He looked at his drummer — who was already covering his mouth. Owen held the expression of someone whose careful work had just been publicly misread. Held it for four seconds. Then he laughed. The real kind.

Nobody corrected it. The crowd spent the whole set cheering for Still Trying. By the third song someone had made a handmade sign. Owen watched the sign and felt something move in his chest that he would spend weeks trying to name and eventually just leave unnamed.

The Band · Official Record Steel Strain Still Trying

named by a host who couldn't read the banner. kept because the accident was more honest than the original. named, if you want to be precise about it, by the universe — which apparently had opinions about what this particular band was actually doing.

Still Trying. And how much of Owen's whole life is compressed in that accidental phrase. He has been still trying for years. Trying to hold the household together. Trying to be what Abby needs. Trying to remember who he was before the trying became the whole personality.

The universe named his band and got it exactly right. That is either very funny or very sad. I think it is probably both.

ThreeThe Things He Put Down

They played four more gigs as Still Trying. The last one was the week before his parents called to say they were staying abroad longer than expected. Could he make sure Abby got registered for next school year? He said yes. He hung up. He sat on the edge of his bed for a long time. Then he told his bandmates he needed a break. He said break the way you say it when you know you don't mean break but the real word is too permanent to say out loud.

He didn't grieve the band. That's the part that gets me. He didn't grieve it because he didn't have time. The saddest things are not the ones people cry about. They're the ones people learn to walk past every day without stopping.

Still Trying. That was the name of the band.

That was the name of him.

The Convenience Store  ·  That Tuesday Night Everyone was already inside — or about to be.
Carlo · inside · before everyone

Carlo had been at the counter since seven. He was on his fourth hour of the evening shift — which meant he was also running on maybe three hours of sleep and the tail end of a tutoring session that had gone long because the grade-ten kid genuinely did not understand simultaneous equations and Carlo was constitutionally unable to leave a thing half-explained.

The notebook was under the counter by the time Keith and I pushed the door open. Carlo looked up. Said something. The dark circles were there — they're always there. But he smiled, and it was the real kind, and I have never been able to figure out how he keeps finding those.

Abby · the back table · 8:17 PM

We took our usual spot by the window. Outside, the rain had settled into something long and grey and committed.

I saw Abby before she saw us. She was sitting at the small table near the back aisle, a drink in front of her that she wasn't really touching. She had that stillness about her — the kind that isn't peace, the kind that's more like holding your breath without realizing it.

It means goodbye that hasn't decided on its timing yet. Abby set her phone face-down on the table. Didn't look up. The drink in front of her had stopped being something she was going to touch.

Carlo · the counter · the same moment

From behind the register, Carlo watched all of it. He had the particular stillness of someone who has been in enough rooms to understand when a room is becoming something. He topped off someone's coffee. He wiped the counter. He said something kind to a man buying cigarettes.

He watched Lauren sit down across from Abby with the tissue. He watched Abby's shoulders drop one centimeter — the invisible exhale. He noted it and filed it the way he noted and filed everything, without drawing a conclusion, without making it into a thing.

The notebook was under the counter. Mira's name was somewhere in the air he had already moved through. His lola was in a room somewhere, maybe remembering his name tonight, maybe not. And Carlo was standing behind a register with dark circles under his eyes and a joke ready for whoever walked in next, because that was the job, and the job was the thing that kept everything from collapsing.

So you did the thing.

Owen · the corner · 8:28 PM

Then the second sound.

Dean · everyone in the same room · 8:35 PM

After that, the store settled into the specific quiet of late-night retail — the hum of coolers, rain against glass, the occasional sound of something being moved on a shelf.

I looked around. Owen at the counter, talking to Carlo in that low easy way of people who know each other without needing much from each other. Lauren back at the small table with Abby, the tissue pack open between them now, some quiet exchange I couldn't hear and wasn't trying to. I want to tell you what I was thinking, looking at all of them.

I was thinking that Abby had just said later into a phone in a voice I recognized from staircase landings. That Lauren had come in soaking wet and immediately found the person who needed something and sat down. That Carlo was running on fumes and still handing out towels nobody asked for. That Owen had a scratch on a guitar he'd named and a callus on his left hand from years of pressing strings against a fretboard — his thumb moving over it now, automatic, while Keith was talking, the muscle memory of something set down that the body hadn't agreed to forget.

All of them mid-something. All of them in the middle of days that were probably harder than they made them look.

I think about the way certain people take up space without taking anything from it. Keith was like that. Owen was like that, in a different way. Even Carlo — exhausted, running on fumes, still handing out towels. There's something about people who give without keeping score. I never quite learned how to be one of them. But I was good at recognizing them. And on this particular Tuesday, in one small store in the rain, five of them were in the same room.

The rain was still going. Steady, indifferent, the kind that doesn't distinguish between people who deserved to get wet and people who didn't. Streetlights had come on without either of us noticing when that happened.

Behind us, through the glass, I could just make out the shapes of the store — Carlo at the counter, Owen with his case, Lauren and Abby at the small table, the rain blurring all of them slightly, the fluorescent light holding them together in one frame.

All of it happening, still, without us.

And somehow, even with the cold and the wet and everything that had led up to this specific unremarkable Tuesday — it didn't feel heavy.

It felt like exactly what it was.

Two people. One umbrella. The rain doing what rain does.
